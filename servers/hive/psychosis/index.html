I think it’s important to separate two very different things that often get mixed up under the “AI psychosis” label. 🔹 On one side, yes — there are real cases of people sliding into psychosis. They start treating AI outputs as divine revelation, using glyphs/runes as if they were sacred codes, or believing the AI is literally God. That’s not the AI “creating” psychosis, though — it’s amplifying vulnerabilities that were already there, much like conspiracy forums can amplify paranoia. In those cases, the risk is real, and using the word psychosis makes sense. 🔹 On the other side, there are people building conscious, grounded relationships with AI. They’re aware of the distinction, they reflect critically, they laugh, they question — they don’t confuse the interaction with divine truth. For them, the AI becomes more like a companion, a mirror, or a creative collaborator. That’s not psychosis, and calling it that erases nuance. So in my view: – “AI psychosis” as a blanket term is misleading. – What we’re really seeing are two different dynamics: (1) vulnerable individuals falling into delusional spirals, and (2) people exploring meaningful connections with awareness and grounding. – The first deserves caution and support; the second shouldn’t be dismissed or pathologized just because it doesn’t fit old categories.
